aria-label "Article content"  section class "body main-article-body"  section class "abstract" id "ab0005"  h2 Abstract /h2   p Ceramides are important for skin health, with a multitude of species found in both dermis and epidermis. The epidermis contains linoleic acid- strong E /strong ster-linked  strong O /strong mega-hydroxylated ceramides of 6- strong H /strong ydroxy-sphingosine,  strong S /strong phingosine and  strong P /strong hytosphingosine bases (CER[ em EOH /em ], CER[ em EOS /em ] and CER[ em EOP /em ], respectively), that are crucial for the formation of the epidermal barrier, conferring protection from environmental factors and preventing trans-epidermal water loss. Furthermore, a large number of ceramides, derivatives of the same sphingoid bases and various fatty acids, are produced by dermal and epidermal cells and perform signalling roles in cell functions ranging from differentiation to apoptosis. /p   p Supplementation with the n-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA) eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA) have shown promise as therapeutic agents in a number of inflammatory skin conditions, altering the lipid profile of the skin and production of bioactive lipids such as the eicosanoids, docosanoids and endocannabinoids. In this study we wished to investigate whether EPA and DHA could also affect the ceramide profile in epidermis and dermis, and, in this way, contribute to formation of a robust lipid barrier and ceramide-mediated regulation of skin functions. /p   p  em Ex vivo /em  skin explants were cultured for 6 days, and supplemented with EPA or DHA (50 μM). Liquid chromatography coupled to tandem mass spectrometry with electrospray ionisation was used to assess the prevalence of 321 individual ceramide species, and a number of sphingoid bases, phosphorylated sphingoid bases, and phosphorylated ceramides, within the dermis and epidermis. /p   p EPA augmented dermal production of members of the ceramide families containing  strong N /strong on-hydroxy fatty acids and  strong S /strong phingosine or  strong D /strong ihydrosphingosine bases (CER[ em NS /em ] and CER[ em NDS /em ], respectively), while epidermal CER[ em EOH /em ], CER[ em EOS /em ] and CER[ em EOP /em ] ceramides were not affected. DHA did not significantly affect ceramide production. Ceramide-1-phosphate levels in the epidermis, but not the dermis, increased in response to EPA, but not DHA. /p   p This  em ex vivo /em  study shows that dietary supplementation with EPA has the potential to alter the ceramide profile of the skin, and this may contribute to its anti-inflammatory profile. This has implications for formation of the epidermal lipid barrier, and signalling pathways within the skin mediated by ceramides and other sphingolipid species. Introduction /h2   section id "s0010"  h3 class "pmc_sec_title" 1.1. Skin physiology is supported by distinct lipid metabolism /h3   p The skin is the largest organ of the body and functions as a barrier preventing water loss and entry of harmful compounds or organisms from the environment, as well as offering protection against solar radiation  a href " bb0005" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0005" [1] /a . It also allows sensations of pain, temperature and touch, contributes to thermoregulation, and mediates inflammatory and immune responses. The skin is characterised by active lipid metabolism, with a distinct profile of lipids, and is the source of an array of bioactive lipid mediators  a href " bb0010" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0010" [2] /a . /p   p Skin is composed of three main layers, the dermis, the epidermis and the hypodermis, and because of its protective function, is continuously dynamic. Epidermal keratinocytes differentiate as they migrate upwards, eventually becoming corneocytes. These protein-enriched corneocytes are embedded in a lipid-rich matrix comprising a large number of ceramide species, cholesterol and free fatty acids, to form the  em stratum corneum /em   a href " bb0015" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0015" [3] /a ,  a href " bb0020" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0020" [4] /a . Also found in the epidermis are the antigen-presenting Langerhans cells and pigment-producing melanocytes  a href " bb0010" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0010" [2] /a . The dermis contains a number of important structures (small blood vessels and nerves, hair follicles and sweat glands) together with dermal fibroblasts and immune cells, all enclosed within collagen and elastic fibres  a href " bb0010" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0010" [2] /a ,  a href " bb0025" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0025" [5] /a . The subcutaneous tissue (hypodermis) that forms the lowest of the skin layers contains blood vessels and adipocytes sufficient in number to form adipose tissue. Although adipose tissue has its major role as a lipid storage site, it is also a source of fatty acid-derived lipid mediators with signalling properties  a href " bb0030" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0030" [6] /a . Sebum-derived lipids are found on the surface of the skin; this complex mixture of triacylglycerols, diacylglycerols, non-esterified fatty acids, wax esters, squalene and cholesterol esters offer photoprotection and exert anti-microbial activities, although the entire spectrum of its role is not yet fully understood  a href " bb0035" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0035" [7] /a ,  a href " bb0040" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0040" [8] /a . /p   p Importantly, it has been shown that epidermal keratinocytes have very low desaturase activity, resulting in poor ability to form long chain polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA), including arachidonic acid (AA; 20:4n-6)  a href " bb0045" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0045" [9] /a . This highlights the importance of systemic long chain PUFA supplementation for skin health, and the role of dermal-epidermal cross talk for the efficient structure and function of the epidermis. Indeed, the essential fatty acid linoleic acid (LA; C18:2n-6) is of particular significance to skin health, as it contributes to the formation of ceramides essential for the structure of the epidermal barrier  a href " bb0015" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0015" [3] /a , and the absence of LA-containing ceramides in the stratum corneum results in barrier permeability problems  a href " bb0050" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0050" [10] /a ,  a href " bb0055" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0055" [11] /a ,  a href " bb0060" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0060" [12] /a . /p   p As well as contributing to the structural integrity of the skin, PUFA such as LA, AA, eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA; C20:5n-3) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA; C22:6n-3) are metabolized to octadecanoids, eicosanoids, docosanoids, endocannabinoids and related bioactive lipid species, known to mediate inflammatory and immune reactions in many tissues, including skin  a href " bb0065" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0065" [13] /a ,  a href " bb0070" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0070" [14] /a ,  a href " bb0075" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0075" [15] /a ,  a href " bb0080" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0080" [16] /a ,  a href " bb0085" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0085" [17] /a . /p  /section  section id "s0015"  h3 class "pmc_sec_title" 1.2. Sphingolipids perform unique roles within the skin /h3   p Comprising both signalling and structural lipids, the cutaneous sphingolipids are crucial for skin health. Sphingoid bases, ceramides and their phosphorylated derivatives have been analysed, and hundreds identified in skin  a href " bb0090" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0090" [18] /a ,  a href " bb0095" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0095" [19] /a ,  a href " bb0100" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0100" [20] /a ,  a href " bb0105" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0105" [21] /a ,  a href " bb0110" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0110" [22] /a ,  a href " bb0115" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0115" [23] /a ,  a href " bb0120" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0120" [24] /a . Whilst the functions of the signalling sphingolipid species in other mammalian tissues continue to be elucidated  a href " bb0125" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0125" [25] /a , knowledge of their function in skin is rather sparse  a href " bb0130" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0130" [26] /a . However, the role of structural ceramides in forming the skin barrier is better understood. /p   p As discussed, the barrier function of the skin depends on the presence of a specific mixture of lipids within the intercellular spaces of the  em stratum corneum /em   a href " bb0135" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0135" [27] /a . Towards the end of the keratinocyte differentiation process, synthesised lipids that have accumulated in lamellar granules are discharged and released by the action of enzymes co-secreted from the lamellar bodies (including phospholipases, sphingomyelinase and β-glucocerebrosidase)  a href " bb0020" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0020" [4] /a ,  a href " bb0140" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0140" [28] /a . These are the main source of the barrier lipids, and diseases with changes in activity of such enzymes give rise to abnormal barrier function (e.g  a href " bb0145" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0145" [29] /a .). Barrier lipids represent about 10  of the mass of the  em stratum corneum /em , and consist of about 50  ceramides, 25  cholesterol and 15  non-esterified fatty acids  a href " bb0150" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0150" [30] /a ,  a href " bb0155" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0155" [31] /a ,  a href " bb0160" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0160" [32] /a . The composition and organisation of these lipids has been discussed recently by van Smeden et al.  a href " bb0165" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0165" [33] /a . /p   p The ceramides are of particular importance, and display extreme complexity due to a multitude of substructures comprising different sphingoid bases and fatty acids (see  a href " f0005" class "usa-link" Fig. 1 /a  for ceramide families, including structures and an explanation of the nomenclature) including some very long chain N-acyl fatty acids. Notably, there is a sub-group of ceramides that contain LA esterified to the omega carbon of omega-hydroxy fatty acids (CER[ em EOS /em ], CER[ em EODS /em ], CER[ em EOP /em ] and CER[ em EOH /em ];  a href " f0005" class "usa-link" Fig. 1 /a ). These are found solely in the epidermis and help to form the multilayers of membrane sheets, crucial for skin barrier function  a href " bb0100" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0100" [20] /a . /p   figure class "fig xbox font-sm" id "f0005" title "Fig. 1"  h4 class "obj_head" Fig. 1. /h4   p class "img-box line-height-none margin-x-neg-2 tablet:margin-x-0 text-center"  a class "tileshop" target "_blank" href "https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title Click 20on 20image 20to 20zoom amp;p PMC3 amp;id 5504780_gr1.jpg"  img class "graphic zoom-in" src "https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/4628/5504780/ba99a7da00b2/gr1.jpg" loading "lazy" height "469" width "758" alt "Fig. 1"  /a  /p   div class "p text-right font-secondary"  a href "figure/f0005/" class "usa-link" target "_blank" rel "noopener noreferrer" Open in a new tab /a  /div   figcaption  p The ceramides are a complex family of sphingoid-based lipids. Nomenclature of the different ceramide families is based on the sphingoid base (sphingosine, S; dihydrosphingosine (also known as sphinganine), DS; phytosphingosine, P; 6-hydroxysphingosine; H) and fatty acid (non-hydroxy, H; alpha-hydroxy, A; ester-linked omega-hydroxy, EO). Different combinations of sphingoid base and fatty acid generate the different ceramide families, for example a non-hydroxy fatty acid (N) amide-linked to a sphingosine (S) base creates a CER[ em NS /em ] ceramide. Individual ceramide species are further identified by carbon chain lengths of both base and fatty acid. For example, an NS ceramide with a 30-carbon non-hydroxy fatty acid and a 20-carbon sphingosine base is denoted as N(30)S(20). The above ceramide families have all been identified in human skin  a href " bb0105" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0105" [21] /a . /p  /figcaption  /figure  p In essential fatty acid deficiency, there is an abnormal appearance of the extracellular lipid membrane that is commensurate with impaired barrier function, which reflects failure to synthesise these ceramide families  a href " bb0170" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0170" [34] /a ,  a href " bb0175" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0175" [35] /a . The important role of LA in barrier function is discussed by Elias et al.  a href " bb0015" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0015" [3] /a . Additionally, changes in individual ceramide proportions have a noticeable effect on barrier conformation and, hence, its properties  a href " bb0180" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0180" [36] /a . /p  /section  section id "s0020"  h3 class "pmc_sec_title" 1.3. n-3PUFA supplementation and skin sphingolipids /h3   p The importance of PUFA in skin health is clear, especially the potential benefits of n-3PUFA supplementation, which has been shown in several studies to ameliorate some inflammatory skin conditions  a href " bb0075" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0075" [15] /a ,  a href " bb0185" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0185" [37] /a ,  a href " bb0190" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0190" [38] /a ,  a href " bb0195" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0195" [39] /a ,  a href " bb0200" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0200" [40] /a . Discussion /h2   p The importance of dietary fatty acids for skin health has long been known  a href " bb0295" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0295" [59] /a ,  a href " bb0300" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0300" [60] /a . N-3PUFA supplementation has been shown to be beneficial for skin health  a href " bb0010" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0010" [2] /a ,  a href " bb0075" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0075" [15] /a ,  a href " bb0185" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0185" [37] /a ,  a href " bb0200" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0200" [40] /a , and since ceramides are crucial in normal skin function, we were interested in whether manipulation of the skin's fatty acid profile following n-3PUFA treatment would have an impact on ceramide production in the dermis and epidermis. We have profiled 321 ceramide species in human dermis and epidermis using a semi-quantitative UPLC/ESI-MS/MS method  a href " bb0080" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0080" [16] /a . Identification of individual ceramide species by fragmentation pattern and retention time has allowed us to perform relative quantification using internal standards  a href " bb0090" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0090" [18] /a ,  a href " bb0095" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0095" [19] /a ,  a href " bb0105" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0105" [21] /a . This analysis includes CER[ em NS /em ], CER[ em NDS /em ], CER[ em NH /em ], CER[ em NP /em ], CER[ em AS /em ], CER[ em ADS /em ], CER[ em AH /em ] and CER[ em AP /em ] species that may have roles in inter-cellular signalling, as well as CER[ em EOS /em ], CER[ em EOH /em ] and CER[ em EOP /em ] species that are crucial for successful epidermal barrier formation  a href " bb0020" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0020" [4] /a ,  a href " bb0130" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0130" [26] /a . In addition, we have examined some of the related bioactive lipid mediators, including S1P and C1P species known to have signalling roles within the skin  a href " bb0130" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0130" [26] /a ,  a href " bb0305" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0305" [61] /a ,  a href " bb0310" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0310" [62] /a . /p   p Several species of CER[ em NS /em ] and CER[ em NDS /em ] showed increased expression in the dermis, but not epidermis, in response to EPA. The ceramide species that increased in response to EPA were among the least abundant of the CER[ em NS /em ] and CER[ em NDS /em ] families ( a href " ec0010" class "usa-link" Supplementary data S2 /a ). Analysis of ceramide family totals showed no significant differences following EPA supplementation (data not shown), and the increases were specific to these low-abundance species. This indicates the response is not an artefactual finding due to low basal amounts of these species. Although a similar trend was observed with DHA supplementation, this did not reach statistical significance for any ceramide species. This was due to variation in the responses of skin from different donors, and is supported by previous reports on inter-individual variation in the uptake and response to n-3PUFA  a href " bb0315" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0315" [63] /a . The n-3PUFA-induced increases were not seen with any of the other ceramide families, and overall, we observed that the skin's ceramide profile was largely unaffected by provision of EPA or DHA. To date, we have been unable to find any reports on the effect of EPA and DHA on ceramides throughout the dermis and epidermis, and this absence of global ceramide changes may highlight the complexity of ceramide metabolism in the skin. /p   p Previous studies on the influence of n-3PUFA on ceramides in tissues other than skin show tissue-dependent responses. In murine muscle cells, EPA or DHA added independently at a concentration of 50 μM have both been shown to impair a palmitate-induced increase in CER[ em NS /em ]  a href " bb0320" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0320" [64] /a , whilst  em in vivo /em  dietary fish oil supplementation (which contains EPA and DHA together) in mice can inhibit a high-fat-diet-induced CER[ em NS /em ] increase in skeletal muscle  a href " bb0325" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0325" [65] /a . It has also been shown in humans that adopting a Nordic diet (which includes high fish consumption), leads to reduction in some plasma CER[ em NS /em ] ceramides, although the originating tissue of these species is unknown  a href " bb0330" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0330" [66] /a . This variation between tissue responses is evident within the same animal, as mice on fish oil- or krill oil-supplemented diets had decreased CER[ em NS /em ] and CER[ em NDS /em ] ceramides in the liver, and increased CER[ em NS /em ] and CER[ em NDS /em ] ceramides in the brain  a href " bb0335" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0335" [67] /a . Increased ceramide production has also been seen in breast cancer cells following EPA or DHA supplementation  em in vitro /em , or fish oil supplementation  em in vivo /em , through increased neutral sphingomyelinase activity  a href " bb0340" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0340" [68] /a . /p   p Given the signalling roles of CER[ em NS /em ] and CER[ em NDS /em ] species, their reported increase in the dermis in response to EPA supplementation may contribute to regulation of fibroblast functions, such as cell differentiation and apoptosis  a href " bb0130" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0130" [26] /a . However, since the identification of individual ceramide species is a relatively recent development in lipidomics, there is little evidence for the roles of specific ceramides, and we have been unable to find any previous studies on the individual CER[ em NS /em ] and CER[ em NDS /em ] species we found to be altered by EPA. As the field advances, we expect studies to be performed using individual ceramide species, which may elucidate their roles. /p   p The involvement of sphingomyelinase is a possible explanation for our findings. Increased sphingomyelinase activity would lead to the increased release of ceramides stored in membrane sphingomyelins ( a href " f0030" class "usa-link" Fig. 6 /a ). However, sphingomyelinase is known to release both CER[ em NS /em ] and CER[ em AS /em ] ceramides from sphingomyelins, in the  em stratum corneum /em  at least  a href " bb0345" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0345" [69] /a , and CER[ em AS /em ] ceramides remained unaltered by n-3PUFA supplementation in our study. We have previously shown that EPA, and its metabolite docosapentaenoic acid, can downregulate neutral sphingomyelinase and ceramide expression in rat brain  a href " bb0350" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0350" [70] /a . DHA has previously been shown to downregulate ceramide release from sphingomyelins in retinal endothelial cells through a downregulation of acid sphingomyelinase  a href " bb0355" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0355" [71] /a . However, the ceramide profile of the skin is unique and the effect of n-3PUFA on sphingomyelinases in the skin may be different. /p   figure class "fig xbox font-sm" id "f0030" title "Fig. 6"  h3 class "obj_head" Fig. 6. /h3   p class "img-box line-height-none margin-x-neg-2 tablet:margin-x-0 text-center"  a class "tileshop" target "_blank" href "https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title Click 20on 20image 20to 20zoom amp;p PMC3 amp;id 5504780_gr6.jpg"  img class "graphic zoom-in" src "https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/4628/5504780/27ed41d712cd/gr6.jpg" loading "lazy" height "968" width "682" alt "Fig. 6"  /a  /p   div class "p text-right font-secondary"  a href "figure/f0030/" class "usa-link" target "_blank" rel "noopener noreferrer" Open in a new tab /a  /div   figcaption  p The sphingolipid cycle allows ceramides to be metabolized through various pathways, including  em de novo /em  synthesis, presence in sphingomyelins or glucosylceramides, or production of phosphorylated signalling species. There are multiple potential targets through which n-3PUFA could alter ceramide levels. /p  /figcaption  /figure  p Another potential store of ceramides in the skin is the glucosylceramides ( a href " f0030" class "usa-link" Fig. 6 /a ). Although CER[ em NS /em ] have been identified as being part of glucosylceramides in the epidermis, CER[ em NDS /em ] ceramides have not to date  a href " bb0360" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0360" [72] /a . The influence of n-3PUFA on the activity of glucosylceramidases, the enzymes that release ceramides from the glucosylceramides, is not known, and further studies would be needed to explore if increased glucosylceramidase activity could explain the increase of only CER[ em NS /em ] and CER[ em NDS /em ] species, as observed in our study. Inhibition of the uptake of ceramides into sphingomyelins or glucosylceramides could also explain our findings, although the regulation of these processes in skin is not yet understood  a href " bb0345" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0345" [69] /a ,  a href " bb0360" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0360" [72] /a . Overall, the impact of n-3PUFA on cutaneous sphingomyelins and glucosylceramides merits further investigation, as effects on the activity of specific sphingomyelinases or glucosylceramidases in our study cannot be ruled out. /p   p A possible mechanism of increased ceramide production could be an effect on  em de novo /em  ceramide biosynthesis ( a href " f0030" class "usa-link" Fig. 6 /a ). CER[ em NDS /em ] ceramides are produced upstream of other ceramides, and initially converted into CER[ em NS /em ] species. DHA, but not EPA, has been found to block western-diet-induced upregulation of  em de novo /em  ceramide synthesis in the liver, by blocking the induction of serine-palmitoyl transferase long chain base subunit-1  a href " bb0365" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0365" [73] /a , but the unique sphingolipid metabolism in the skin could lead to different responses compared with other tissues. /p   p The absence of any change in CER[ em AS /em ], CER[ em ADS /em ], CER[ em AH /em ] or CER[ em AP /em ] ceramides is notable, since it indicates that only ceramides containing a non-hydroxy fatty acid are affected by short-term n-3PUFA supplementation. There is evidence that n-3PUFA preferentially displace certain fatty acids from cell membranes, and ceramide-specific changes might be related to this  a href " bb0370" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0370" [74] /a . Previous studies on the effect of n-3PUFA on ceramides in tissues other than skin have focussed on CER[ em NS /em ] and CER[ em NDS /em ] species  a href " bb0320" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0320" [64] /a ,  a href " bb0325" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0325" [65] /a ,  a href " bb0335" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0335" [67] /a , and so more studies are needed to explore this. The levels of epidermal CER[ em EOS /em ], CER[ em EOH /em ] and CER[ em EOP /em ] ceramides were not altered either. These ceramide families are crucial in the formation of the epidermal barrier in the stratum corneum, and are unique in their requirement for LA  a href " bb0100" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0100" [20] /a . LA deficiency results in substitution with oleic or other fatty acids instead  a href " bb0375" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0375" [75] /a . Our results suggest that short-term n-3PUFA supplementation did not affect the availability of linoleic acid for continued synthesis of these species. However, since we have used a targeted approach to measure ceramides in this assay, we only evaluated a fixed panel of ceramide species. This means there could be new species formed following EPA or DHA supplementation that we did not identify. Examples of new species could include acyl-ceramides containing alpha-linolenic acid (ALA) instead of LA, should EPA or DHA undergo retroconversion to ALA (which has previously been observed in the blood  a href " bb0380" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0380" [76] /a ) in a similar manner that AA can undergo retroconversion to LA in the skin  a href " bb0385" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0385" [77] /a . /p   p While we observed no change in the sphingoid bases or phosphorylated sphingoid bases measured following n-3PUFA supplementation, an increase in epidermal, although not dermal, C1P species following EPA treatment was noted. C1P species are synthesised from ceramides by ceramide kinase  a href " bb0305" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0305" [61] /a . As we did not measure a significant increase in epidermal precursor ceramides, our findings could be attributed to increased ceramide kinase activity. Such enhanced activity could also explain the lack of increased levels of ceramides in the EPA-treated epidermis, as metabolism to C1P species would negate their accumulation ( a href " f0030" class "usa-link" Fig. 6 /a ). Increased levels of C1P in the epidermis could have an anti-inflammatory effect, stimulating cell proliferation and migration, and inhibiting apoptosis  a href " bb0130" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0130" [26] /a ,  a href " bb0305" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0305" [61] /a . /p   p A valuable aspect of this study was the independent assessment of EPA and DHA, two n-3PUFA that are often considered together in nutritional studies ( em e.g. /em  assessing intake of fish or fish oil). The different response of skin sphingolipids to EPA and DHA support the notion that their action relates to formation of specific lipid mediators, rather than n-3PUFA-related changes in membrane composition. For example, the endocannabinoid receptor CB1 is linked to ceramide synthesis through sphingomyelin hydrolysis and  em de novo /em  synthesis  a href " bb0390" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0390" [78] /a , and we have previously shown that supplementation of  em ex vivo /em  skin with EPA or DHA increased production of the endocannabinoid anandamide, as well as the EPA- and DHA-derived congeners  a href " bb0080" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0080" [16] /a . Indeed, in an earlier study using the same  em ex vivo /em  organ culture model we found that following just 3 days of supplementation with EPA or DHA the skin started to produce EPA- and DHA-derived eicosanoid and N-acyl ethanolamide species, respectively, in both the dermis and epidermis. This indicates that not only was the skin able to take up the PUFA and distribute them throughout the skin, but also metabolise the EPA and DHA into signalling compounds  a href " bb0080" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0080" [16] /a . /p   p The different responses of the dermis and epidermis reported in this study could indicate compartmental differences in lipid metabolism. Whilst CER[ em NS /em ] and CER[ em NDS /em ] ceramides were increased in the dermis but not epidermis in response to EPA ( a href " f0020" class "usa-link" Fig. 4 /a ), C1P species were increased in the epidermis but not the dermis ( a href " f0025" class "usa-link" Fig. 5 /a ). In the skin organ culture method employed in this study, n-3PUFA supplementation was through the dermis that was submerged in culture medium, replicating the  em in vivo /em  support of the epidermis. Skin was harvested after 6 days in culture, which we have previously shown is sufficient for dermal and epidermal uptake and metabolism of supplementary n-3PUFA  a href " bb0080" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0080" [16] /a  (and shown in  a href " ec0015" class "usa-link" Supplementary data S3 /a ). Therefore, differences in enzyme expression between the two skin compartments could contribute to different responses to n-3PUFA, although to-date the epidermis has been studied far more extensively than the dermis, and no comprehensive comparison of sphingolipid enzyme expression is available. /p   p In light of differences seen in this study, an important aspect is the examination of the dermis and epidermis separately. We believe that this is the first investigation of the full ceramide profile and comparison of both skin layers. The different responses of the main skin compartments to n-3PUFA supplementation reveal the importance of studying full-thickness skin, since cross-talk and communication between the epidermal and dermal layers could have significant roles to play in skin health and disease  a href " bb0395" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0395" [79] /a . The differences are highlighted when the proportions of ceramide families are compared with those found in previous studies that focussed on the stratum corneum  a href " bb0105" class "usa-link" aria-describedby "bb0105" [21] /a . Whilst tape-stripping of the stratum corneum is a useful, non-invasive sampling technique that allows analysis of the surface ceramides, it does not accurately reflect the profile of the dermis and epidermis, and biopsy sampling is the best approach to study full-thickness skin ceramides. /p   p Overall, this  em ex vivo /em  study has implications for our understanding of the role of n-3PUFA in skin health. The implications of EPA-induced ceramide changes in the skin should be explored further, and studies are needed to directly assess other aspects of skin physiology in this skin organ culture model following EPA supplementation, since we limited our analysis to ceramide profiling. Whilst the skin organ culture system attempts to replicate the systemic delivery and metabolism of n-3PUFA in the epidermis and dermis, clinical studies with dietary supplementation are needed to confirm whether their effects on cutaneous ceramides are reproducible  em in vivo, /em  and allow the detailed examination of the impact of longer-term dietary supplementation on skin lipid biology. /p   p The following are the supplementary data related to this article. /p   section class "sm xbox font-sm" id "ec0005"  div class "caption p"   span Table S1 /span  p MRM transitions and conditions for sphingolipid species analysis by UPLC/ESI-MS/MS. /p   /div   div class "media p" title "Table S1"  div class "caption"   a href "/articles/instance/5504780/bin/mmc1.docx" data-ga-action "click_feat_suppl" class "usa-link" mmc1.docx /a  sup  (56.4KB, docx)  /sup   /div  /div  /section  section class "sm xbox font-sm" id "ec0010"  div class "caption p"   span Table S2 /span  p Concentrations of sphingolipid species in the dermis and epidermis, and their log2 fold change following supplementation with EPA and DHA.
